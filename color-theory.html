Color Theory Overview

According to the Interaction Design Foundation, Color Theory refers to the "study of how colors work together and how they affect our emotions and perceptions."

Below is a list of common definitions used in Color Theory.

  1. Primary colors ‐ pure colors, cannot be created by mixing colors together — includes red, yellow, and blue.
  2. Secondary colors ‐ created by mixing two primary colors together — includes green, violet, and orange.
  3. Tertiary colors ‐ created by mixing a primary color and a secondary color — includes blue‐violet, blue‐green, yellow‐green, yellow‐orange, red‐orange, and red‐violet.
  4. Triadic colors ‐ colors that are spaced out in even increments on the color wheel, such as: blue, yellow, and red.
  5. Complementary colors ‐ colors that are opposites on the color wheel, such as: blue and orange.
  6. Split colors ‐ a base color and two colors that would be balanced on the opposite side of the color wheel.
  7. Analogous colors ‐ colors that share a space on the color wheel, such as: orange, red-orange, and red.
  8. Saturation ‐ the degree of purity, brightness, and vividness within a color.
  9. Hue ‐ the purest form of a color.
  10. Tint ‐ created when white is mixed into a color.
  11. Shade ‐ created when black is mixed into a color.
  12. Value ‐ the relative lightness or darkness of a color.
  13. Contrasting ‐ difference in hue.
  14. Vibrancy ‐ in respect to color, vibrancy is a lively, bright, rich, and energetic hue.
  15. Neutral colors ‐ often perceived as the absence of color, they actually typically contain subtle undertones.
  16. Warm colors ‐ bring energy and intensity to a design, and can often invoke strong emotions such as excitement or warmth — include red, orange, and yellow.
  17. Cool colors ‐ contribute to a calm and soothing atmosphere, and can invoke feelings of peace and tranquility — include blue, purple, and green.
  18. Monochromatic ‐ colors very blended, with minimal hue variety.
  19. Color Scheme ‐ combination of all colors used within a project, arranged in a manner that enhances the overall design.
  20. Whitespace ‐ empty space.
