Color Theory refers to the study of how colors work together to influence perceptions and evoke emotions.

Definitions

  1. Primary colors ‐ pure; cannot be created by mixing other colors. The three primary colors are red, blue, and yellow.
  2. Secondary colors ‐ formed by mixing two primary colors. The three secondary colors are green, violet, andorange.
  3. Tertiary colors ‐ formed by mixing a primary color with a secondary color. Some examples of tertiary colors include blue‐violet, yellow‐green, and red‐orange.
  4. Triadic colors ‐ colors that are spaced out in even increments on the color wheel, such as red , and blue.
  5. Complementary colors ‐ colors that are opposites on the color wheel, such as blue and orange.
  6. Split colors ‐ a base color and two colors that would be balanced on the opposite side of the color wheel. An example would be a base color of red — the split‐complements would be blue‐green and yellow‐green.1
  7. Analogous colors ‐ colors that share a space on the color wheel, such as orange, red‐orange, and red.
  8. Saturation ‐ the degree of purity, brightness, and vividness within a color.
  9. Hue ‐ the purest form of a color.
  10. Tint ‐ created when white is mixed into a color.
  11. Shade ‐ created when black is mixed into a color.
  12. Value ‐ the relative lightness or darkness of a color.
  13. Contrast ‐ difference in hue.
  14. Vibrancy ‐ refers to the intensity of a color. A high-vibrancy color is saturated and bold, while a low-vibrancy color is more muted.
  15. Monochormatic ‐ colors very blended with minimal hue variety.
  16. Color scheme ‐ combination of all colors used within a project, arranged in a manner that enhances the overall design.
  17. Neutral colors ‐ often perceived as the absence of color, they actually typically contain subtle undertones. Some examples of neutral colors include gray and beige.
  18. Warm colors ‐ bring energy and intensity to a design and can often invoke strong emotions. Some examples of warm colors include red and orange.
  19. Cool colors ‐ contribute to a calm and soothing atmosphere and can invoke feelings of peace and tranquility. Some examples of cool colors include blue and purple.
  20. Whitespace ‐ empty space.

Color Theory in Web Design

When it comes to web design, color goes beyond being simply an aesthetic choice — it is a tool used to evoke strong emotions, influence perceptions, and assists in setting the tone of a project. This article explores the role of color in two significantly differing websites: Dashing Diva, a DIY nail/beauty brand, and Spotify, a global music‐streaming platform.

Dashing Diva's UI utilizes bright and muted colors. The white background permits the vibrant colors and unique designs of the products to "pop" against the page. The black text against the white also provides sufficient contrast, making the words legible and accessible while still allowing the products to shine.

Additionally, the pastel pink used for the header and footer bar aids in establishing a feminine tone.

Spotify's UI is a case study in brand recognition. Its logo features an instantly recognizable color — Spotify green.

Source

As per the official guidelines laid out by Spotify, “the green logo should only be used on a black or white background,” allowing the primary green colorway to take center stage. Furthermore, the minimal palette and dark background help facilitate an immersive environment, allowing the focus to remain on the service (music streaming) in contrast to Dashing Diva, where the focus is on the variety of products.

In essence, color is used efficiently on each website to reflect their differing strategic goals. Dashing Diva uses minimal, muted colors in the UI to allow the merchandise to take center stage, while Spotify utilizes color for brand recognition and usability.
